Science on the Side of Scripture: How Physics Supports Providence

Religion, Philosophy, Physics, Quantum Mechanics

Introduction

Advances in scientific fields like physics and cosmology throughout history have either challenged or complemented theological perspectives on the nature of reality. Both science and religion attempt to explain fundamental questions about existence, origin, and meaning. While science relies on empirical evidence and experimentation, religion builds upon faith and revelation. However, when approached with an open mind, scientific discoveries can sometimes strengthen religious convictions.

The connection between science and religion lies in their shared quest for truth and understanding of the world. As scientific knowledge has increased over time, some discoveries have posed challenges to traditional theological doctrines. Yet other findings have unexpectedly aligned with and even supported certain religious perspectives. When viewed with nuance and nuanced skepticism, neither completely confirming nor discounting religious beliefs, scientific discoveries can enrich faith rather than undermine it. This paper will explore how recent physics and quantum mechanics research related to the Big Bang theory, the second law of thermodynamics, and quantum mechanics points to evidence that could strengthen a Christian perspective on the existence of God and ultimate creation and purpose.

Advances in physics and quantum mechanics can strengthen faith in God when viewed with appropriate caveats. The origin and fine-tuning of the universe as described by cosmological and physical theories provide grounds for philosophical arguments for a Creator. While science cannot prove God, it can point to evidence that complements and resonates with theistic theology when understood in context. As we explore new frontiers of physics, an open mind and humble spirit allow both science and faith to deepen our understanding of truth and reality.

Philosophy

The cosmological argument reasons that the origin and order of the universe points to a Creator. The beginning of the universe described by the Big Bang theory supports the notion of a First Cause beyond the physical world. Since something cannot come from nothing, a transcendent force or being would be needed to initiate the original explosion that started the cosmic expansion.

The teleological argument reasons that the purposeful design seen across nature indicates a purposeful Designer. The fine-tuning of universal constants and conditions to allow for complex life suggests intent. If the Big Bang had resulted in even slightly different physical parameters like the strength of the four fundamental forces, planets and biological organisms as we know them could not exist. The precise conditions necessary for life to exist point beyond mere chance and natural laws to a purposeful Creator.

While both arguments have been criticized for lacking empirical proof, from a Christian perspective they form reasonable philosophical inferences that align with biblical teachings on creation. Taken together, they point to God as a transcendent First Cause and intelligent Designer of the universe and life. As the cosmological and teleological arguments strengthen each other, so too scientific discoveries about the origin and fine-tuning of the universe can lend credence to traditional theological views of God as Creator. However, philosophy and science each have limits in comprehensively explaining ultimate origins. Only through divine revelation do Christians fully understand the ultimate Author of creation.

Physics and the Origin of the universe

The account of creation in Genesis describes how God created the heavens and the earth. The physical universe came into being in an ordered sequence over six days by direct divine fiat. On the first three days, God created light, the firmament dividing the waters above from below, and the dry land appearing with vegetation. On the next three days, God created the luminaries like the sun, moon and stars, aquatic and avian creatures, land animals, and lastly humans made in his image.

While the Big Bang theory postulates that the universe began in a hot, dense state over 13 billion years ago, there are good reasons to doubt this model from a biblical perspective. The Bible teaches that the earth was created before the stars and that the earth stands fixed while the heavenly bodies revolve around it. The sun, moon and stars serve to mark days, seasons and years for life on earth rather than the reverse. So rather than the universe beginning with a cosmic explosion resulting in the earth, the earth appears to be the primary focus around which God created the celestial realm.

The second law of thermodynamics, while showing that the physical universe tends towards disorder over time, does not contradict the miraculous divine creation described in Genesis. God created a perfect, well-ordered universe in the beginning and imbued it with functional natural laws. The processes of decay and entropy we now observe do not negate God's original creative act. Rather, they show God continues to sustain the universe moment by moment despite the curse of decay after human sin entered the world.

In summary, biblical creationism points to an intelligent Designer and ultimate Creator who directly formed the earth and heavens by divine fiat. The Genesis account indicates God created an orderly, functional universe designed for human life from the beginning. While natural laws like thermodynamics operate consistently, the processes of the physical universe should be interpreted through a biblical worldview that recognizes God's supernatural creative power and providential sustaining work.

Quantum mechanics

Even as classical physics describes natural laws and mechanisms that govern the large-scale universe, quantum mechanics reveals mysterious realities at the subatomic level. Several key aspects of quantum theory point beyond the material world to spiritual realities that complement Christian theology:

The uncertainty principle shows that particles do not have definite properties until measured. This indeterminism suggests the material world is incomplete without an Observer - in this case, God. From a Christian perspective, God transcends the physical realm he created and is actively involved in sustaining and observing it.

Many scientists believe consciousness cannot be fully explained by physical or biological mechanisms. Quantum mechanics shows how consciousness may interact with and collapse quantum possibilities into actualities. Since the human mind appears able to influence physical reality in ways that defy classical determinism, this indicates human beings are more than mere material organisms. The Christian view of the soul affirms humans have nonphysical aspects that image the divine Creator.

The fine-tuning of universal constants and conditions also suggests the universe was designed for conscious observers. The anthropic principle describes how the strengths of fundamental forces and constants appear finely-tuned to allow for the emergence of intelligent life. While multiverse theories attempt a purely materialist explanation, they fail to account for the underlying reason our universe allows for observers at all. For Christians, this points to a Creator who designed the cosmos for the purpose of relating to humanity.

In summary, quantum mechanics reveals a physical world that is strange, indeterminate and suggests the need for an Observer/Consciousness beyond matter itself. Such findings add credibility to Christian theology which affirms God the Creator and Redeemer who sustains and relates to his creation. While science cannot prove metaphysical realities like the human soul or God's existence, quantum theory points beyond the material world in ways that resonate with Christian teachings on creation, providence and redemption.

Conclusion

This paper has explored the biblical account of creation and how it differs from the standard cosmology postulated by modern physics. The Genesis narrative describes a geocentric view of the universe where God created the earth first and then formed the sun, moon and stars to mark seasons and years for life on earth. In this perspective, the earth is stationary at the center of God's creation while the heavenly bodies rotate above on the firmament in orbits surrounding our world.

Rather than originating in a Big Bang around 13 billion years ago, the universe began when God commanded light to shine out of darkness nearly 6,000 years ago according to biblical chronology. The sun, moon and stars were created to give light to the earth and to serve as signs and for seasons, days and years. They circuit the earth in Regular paths, likely in spiral orbits on the firmament above the earth.

The second law of thermodynamics, while showing inherent decay and disorder in the physical universe, does not contradict the supernatural creative act of God or the ordered structure he imposed upon his creation. Natural laws operate consistently but should be viewed through a biblical worldview that recognizes God's creative power and providential control over everything in the universe.

In conclusion, the Genesis account best explains the origin and structure of the universe with God at the center. While modern physics postulates a universe originating billions of years ago in a big explosion, the biblical narrative describes a universe thousands of years old created in an ordered sequence by God for the purpose of sustaining life on earth. With humility and openness, Christians should interpret science through Scripture rather than reshaping their faith to fit the latest theories. As we develop a biblical cosmology, we can only truly comprehend the glory and wisdom of God through his own self-revelation in scripture.
